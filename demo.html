Applicable Legal Framework

The Federal Skilled Worker Program is governed by Division 1 of Part 6 of the Regulations. An applicant must demonstrate that she meets the minimum eligibility requirements set out in section 75 of the Regulations before her application may be assessed under the selection grid established in section 76.

Minimum Eligibility Requirements

Section 75(1) of the Regulations provides that a foreign national is a skilled worker if she has at least one year of continuous full-time (or equivalent part-time) paid work experience within the last ten years in an occupation classified under Skill Type 0, or Skill Level A or B of the National Occupational Classification.

Immigration and Refugee Protection Regulations, SOR/2002-227, s. 75(1): "For the purposes of subsection 12(2) of the Act, a foreign national is a skilled worker if (a) within the 10 years preceding the date of their application for permanent residence, they have at least one year of continuous full-time employment experience, or the equivalent in continuous part-time employment..."

IRPR, s. 75(1)(a)

Additionally, the applicant must demonstrate minimum language proficiency as established by the Minister, and must have her foreign educational credentials assessed by a designated organization.

"A foreign national who is a skilled worker must [...] have their foreign educational credentials assessed by an organization or institution designated under subsection 75(3) as being equivalent to a completed Canadian educational credential."

IRPR, s. 75(2)(d)

Selection Criteria and Points Grid

Once minimum eligibility is established, the applicant is assessed against the six selection factors enumerated in section 76(1) of the Regulations. The current pass mark is 67 points out of a possible 100. The factors and their maximum point values are as follows:

  1. Education (maximum 25 points): Points are awarded based on the level of educational attainment, with a Master's degree at a university attracting 23 points and a doctoral degree attracting the full 25.
  2. Official Languages Proficiency (maximum 28 points): Points are allocated based on the applicant's scores in the four language abilities (reading, writing, listening, speaking) in one or both of Canada's official languages.
  3. Work Experience (maximum 15 points): Points increase with the number of years of qualifying full-time work experience, from 9 points for one year to the maximum 15 for six or more years.
  4. Age (maximum 12 points): The maximum is awarded to applicants between the ages of 18 and 35, with points decreasing by one for each year over 35.
  5. Arranged Employment (maximum 10 points): Points are awarded if the applicant has a valid job offer from a Canadian employer supported by a positive Labour Market Impact Assessment or an exemption therefrom.
  6. Adaptability (maximum 10 points): Points are awarded for factors including prior Canadian work or study experience, having relatives in Canada, and the spouse's education or language ability.

It should be noted that the selection grid operates independently of the Comprehensive Ranking System ("CRS") used within the Express Entry system to rank candidates for invitations to apply. An applicant must meet the 67-point threshold under section 76 to be eligible, but her actual ranking in the Express Entry pool depends on her CRS score, which incorporates additional factors such as skills transferability and Canadian job offers.

Jurisprudence

The Federal Court has consistently held that officers assessing FSWP applications must evaluate the evidence as a whole and may not reject an application on the basis of a single deficiency without considering the totality of the record. In Malik v. Canada (Citizenship and Immigration), Justice Mosley emphasized the importance of a holistic assessment:

"The officer's duty is to weigh the evidence in its entirety and to assess whether, on a balance of probabilities, the applicant has demonstrated that she possesses the qualifications claimed. An officer who focuses on a single perceived weakness to the exclusion of the broader evidentiary record commits a reviewable error."

Malik v. Canada (Citizenship and Immigration), 2014 FC 990 at para 28

Similarly, in Chen v. Canada (Minister of Citizenship and Immigration), the Court found that an officer's failure to consider the applicant's entire professional history, including supervisory responsibilities and progressive career advancement, rendered the decision unreasonable.

"Where the evidence clearly demonstrates a pattern of increasing responsibility and skill development over many years, it is unreasonable for the officer to dismiss the applicant's claimed experience on the basis of minor inconsistencies in job descriptions."

Chen v. Canada (MCI), 2016 FC 702 at para 41

Additional Submissions

Admissibility

The Applicant has no criminal record in any jurisdiction. She has obtained police clearance certificates from Colombia (where she has resided for the majority of her adult life) and from Canada (covering her period of residence in Toronto). Both certificates confirm that no criminal record exists. The Applicant has never been refused a visa or denied entry to any country, and has no history of immigration violations.

With respect to medical admissibility, the Applicant completed her immigration medical examination on March 28, 2024, with Dr. Patricia Alvarez, a designated panel physician in Bogota. The results of the examination have been transmitted directly to Immigration, Refugees and Citizenship Canada through the eMedical system. The Applicant is in good health and has no medical conditions that would render her inadmissible under section 38 of the Act.

Immigration and Refugee Protection Act, S.C. 2001, c. 27, s. 38(1): "A foreign national is inadmissible on health grounds if their health condition (a) is likely to be a danger to public health; (b) is likely to be a danger to public safety; or (c) might reasonably be expected to cause excessive demand on health or social services."

IRPA, s. 38(1)

Settlement Funds

The Applicant is required to demonstrate that she possesses sufficient settlement funds as prescribed by the Regulations. For a single applicant with no dependents, the current minimum threshold is CAD $14,690. The Applicant's bank statements, included at Tab 10, demonstrate a current balance equivalent to approximately CAD $62,000 in her principal savings account, along with an additional CAD $28,000 in a registered investment portfolio. These funds have been accumulated over many years of professional employment and are fully documented.

The Applicant's financial documentation includes six months of consecutive bank statements from Banco de Bogota, a letter from her financial advisor confirming her investment holdings, and a sworn statutory declaration attesting that the funds are unencumbered and available for use upon arrival in Canada.

Genuine Intention to Reside in Canada

The Applicant has a genuine and well-established intention to settle permanently in Canada. Her motivations include the following:

  • She has direct personal experience living and working in Canada, having spent twelve months in Toronto and developed a strong affinity for Canadian society and culture.
  • Her sister and closest family member, Maria, is a Canadian citizen who has offered to assist with the Applicant's initial settlement in Vancouver.
  • Canada's technology sector offers significant career opportunities that align with her professional expertise and ambitions, particularly in the areas of cloud computing and artificial intelligence.
  • The Applicant has already begun researching housing options in the Greater Vancouver Area and has connected with professional networking groups in the local tech community.
"An applicant's expressed intention to reside in Canada must be assessed in light of all the evidence, including ties to Canada, career prospects, family connections, and the overall coherence of the settlement plan."

Operations Manual OP 6A, s. 5.27

Conclusion

For the reasons set out above, the Applicant respectfully submits that her application for permanent residence under the Federal Skilled Worker Program should be approved. The evidence demonstrates, clearly and convincingly, that she meets and exceeds every minimum eligibility requirement prescribed by the Act and the Regulations. Her projected selection score of 84 points surpasses the pass mark by a wide margin. She is admissible on all grounds, possesses settlement funds far in excess of the minimum, and has articulated a genuine, well-supported intention to establish herself permanently in Canada.

The Applicant is precisely the kind of highly skilled, experienced, and motivated individual that the Federal Skilled Worker Program was designed to welcome. We trust that the officer reviewing this application will agree that approval is warranted on the merits.
